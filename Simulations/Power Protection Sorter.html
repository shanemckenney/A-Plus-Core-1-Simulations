Domain 3.6

How to Use This Simulation

Situation

You're picking up the pieces after a storm knocked out power to the Riverside office. Read the incident summary, then decide how every device in the inventory should be protected before the next outage.

How to Use This Simulator

  1. Drag each device from the inventory list into a protection zone.
  2. Click a placed device to send it back to the inventory list if you change your mind.
  3. Click Evaluate Classification to submit your protection plan.
  4. Any device you got wrong will show what actually would have happened to it in a storm like this one — that's your damage report.
  5. Use Show Rationale any time for the general reasoning behind each category.

Why This Matters

On the job, nobody hands you a labeled list. You look at what a device actually does — does it hold live data that outlasts a click, or does it just need clean, spike-free power — and protect it accordingly.

Devices to Classify
Incident Summary: Two nights ago a severe thunderstorm moved through the area and lightning struck a transformer near the building. The office lost power for about 40 minutes, and the grid surged twice as it came back online. The ISP modem's power adapter didn't survive the surge — it had been plugged straight into the wall with nothing protecting it, and now the whole office is waiting on a replacement. Everything else in the office happened to make it through, but only by luck. Before another storm rolls through, sort the full device inventory into the protection it actually needs.

Device Inventory

Drag every device in the inventory into a protection zone → Click Evaluate Classification.
General Reasoning:
  • UPS: Devices where a sudden power loss means lost data or a dropped connection — needs battery runtime to shut down clean or ride out a short outage.
  • Surge Protector: Devices with nothing to lose on a power cut, but still worth protecting from voltage spikes.
  • Wall Outlet: High-draw or heating devices whose load isn't suited to a UPS at all.
